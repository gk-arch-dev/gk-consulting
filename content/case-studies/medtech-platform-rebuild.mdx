The client is a European medtech company that builds software for patient data management in clinical settings. Their existing records platform had been built by a small team over several years and had reached the limits of its maintainability — the audit trail was incomplete for regulatory purposes, the architecture was a monolith that made GDPR data-subject requests slow and error-prone, and the frontend was impossible to evolve without deep backend changes.

I joined as embedded tech lead with a team of six engineers. The rebuild followed a domain-driven microservices approach: a patient identity service, a records service, an audit service with immutable event storage, and a GDPR compliance service that handled data subject requests (access, correction, deletion) as first-class domain operations. All services ran on ECS on AWS with RDS PostgreSQL. Infrastructure was in Terraform. The React TypeScript frontend was designed from the start to be independent of any single backend service.

The platform launched fourteen months after the first commit with full medical-device-grade audit trail coverage and zero major production defects. *The hardest architectural decision*: representing deletion as a domain event rather than a database DELETE — the GDPR right-to-be-forgotten required that we could demonstrate erasure without losing the audit record of the erasure request. This required a custom audit event model that the regulatory reviewers eventually accepted after one revision cycle.
