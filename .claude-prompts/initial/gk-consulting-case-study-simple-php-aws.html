Legacy PHP Monolith → Cloud-Native on AWS.

Eighteen months. Two hundred thousand active users. A PHP monolith carrying a decade of accumulated debt — modernized into a Kotlin and Spring Boot platform on AWS, with a zero-downtime cutover and not one missed product release along the way.

Role Tech Lead
Client German Real Estate Co.
Duration 18 months
Team 5 engineers
Outcome
200K+ active users migrated without service interruption
0 minutes of unplanned downtime during the cutover window
~40% reduction in monthly infrastructure spend
The story

What we did.

The client runs one of Germany's larger lead-generation platforms in the moving-and-relocation space. The product was profitable, but the codebase — a PHP monolith built up over roughly a decade — had become a hiring problem and a delivery problem. The 40-minute deploy pipeline, the cross-team coordination needed for any real change, and the dread around on-call rotations were all pointing to the same conclusion: modernize, but without freezing the roadmap.

I led the migration as Tech Lead with a team of five. We adopted the Strangler Fig pattern — keeping the PHP monolith in production while carving out bounded contexts as Kotlin services on AWS, one at a time. Two architectural decisions shaped everything else: Kotlin over Java (the team wanted something modern, and the JVM kept everything compatible), and event-driven seams over shared databases (legacy and new services exchanged state via SQS and SNS, never direct writes — this is what made incremental migration safe).

Six bounded contexts migrated over eighteen months, ending in a zero-downtime cutover. The platform now runs on a serverless-first architecture (Lambda, DynamoDB, ECS Fargate) with infrastructure managed in CDK. The team grew, the deploy pipeline shrank from 40 minutes to under 8, and the on-call rotation stopped being a punishment. One honest note in retrospect: I'd build observability in from day one next time. We retrofitted distributed tracing and structured logging in month four — work that would have taken half the time if we'd done it earlier.
