Legacy PHP Monolith → Cloud-Native on AWS.

Eighteen months. Two hundred thousand active users. A PHP monolith carrying a decade of accumulated debt — modernized into a Kotlin and Spring Boot platform on AWS, with a zero-downtime cutover and not one missed product release along the way.

Role Tech Lead
Client German Real Estate Co.
Duration 18 months
Team 5 engineers
Outcome
200K+ active users migrated without service interruption
0 minutes of unplanned downtime during the cutover window
~40% reduction in monthly infrastructure spend
01. Context

A profitable platform, slowly suffocating.

The client runs one of Germany's larger lead-generation platforms in the moving-and-relocation space, connecting consumers with vetted service providers. The product is mature, well-known in the market, and consistently profitable — but its technical foundation hadn't kept pace with its commercial ambition.

The codebase was a PHP monolith written over roughly a decade by a rotating cast of engineers. By the time we engaged, the deployment pipeline took 40 minutes, even simple feature work needed cross-team coordination, and on-call rotations were dreaded. Critically, hiring had become a problem: senior engineers no longer wanted to work in legacy PHP, and the existing team was burning out maintaining infrastructure they hadn't designed.

The business goal was unambiguous: modernize without losing momentum. The product team had a roadmap of features the market wanted, and the executive team would not accept a migration that froze that roadmap.

02. Challenge

Three constraints, one architecture.

The technical problem was not unusual. The constraints around it were what made this engagement difficult.

First, no feature freeze. Most monolith-to-cloud migrations buy a window of feature stagnation in exchange for the rewrite. We had no such window. The roadmap kept moving, and the migration had to move with it.

Second, a small team. Five engineers, including myself. Half the team was new to AWS at depth. The architecture we picked needed to be teachable as we built it, not just defensible on paper.

Third, a hard data continuity requirement. The platform sits at the centre of legally-binding lead-handoff transactions. Lost data meant lost revenue and, in the worst cases, regulatory exposure. The migration could not afford even a soft data inconsistency window.

03. Approach

Strangler Fig, with deliberate edges.

We adopted the Strangler Fig pattern — but treated the seams between old and new as first-class architectural elements, not afterthoughts. The PHP monolith stayed in production the entire time. New capabilities and rewritten capabilities were carved out as Kotlin services behind API Gateway, with carefully-designed event boundaries between the old and new worlds.

Below are the four decisions that shaped the migration. Each one had a counter-argument we genuinely considered.

Decision 01

Kotlin over Java for new services

Half the team had Java experience but wanted to learn something new. Kotlin's null safety, data classes, and coroutines reduced boilerplate significantly without breaking JVM compatibility. The cost was a 2-week ramp-up; the benefit was four engineers who never wanted to go back.

Decision 02

Event-driven seams (SQS + SNS)

Old PHP and new Kotlin services exchanged state via async events, never direct database writes. This decoupling let us migrate one bounded context at a time without distributed transactions or schema-sharing pain.

Decision 03

Serverless-first for new bounded contexts

Lambda + DynamoDB for low-traffic admin and reporting paths; ECS Fargate where steady-state load justified it. We avoided the temptation to put everything on Kubernetes — the team didn't need that complexity, and the bill would have doubled.

Decision 04

CDK over Terraform for infrastructure

The team already wrote TypeScript on the frontend; CDK kept infrastructure in a familiar language and unlocked higher-level abstractions. If the team had been Python-first, I'd have made a different call.

04. What shipped

Eighteen months, six milestones.

Month 01–03

Foundation & first carve-out

AWS account hardening (IAM, VPC, KMS, Secrets Manager). CDK scaffolding, CI/CD pipelines. The first bounded context — admin reporting — extracted from the monolith and rebuilt as Lambda + DynamoDB. Proof that the seam worked.

Month 04–07

Lead intake pipeline

Migrated the highest-volume domain — consumer lead intake and provider matching — to a Kotlin/Spring Boot service on ECS Fargate. Event bridge from PHP to Kotlin via SQS. Frontend rewritten in React/TypeScript, served from CloudFront.

Month 08–11

Provider portal

The provider-facing portal — quoting, scheduling, settlement — rebuilt as a separate Kotlin service. Introduced Testcontainers and Wiremock to the team's testing toolkit. Direct client-stakeholder communication channel established.

Month 12–14

Identity & auth consolidation

Cognito-backed user identity, replacing custom session management spread across the monolith. SSO for staff, federated identity for partner providers.

Month 15–17

Final domain & cutover preparation

The remaining commerce/billing domain migrated. Parallel-run validation with continuous diffing between old and new outputs. Runbook and rollback strategy drafted, reviewed, rehearsed.

Month 18

Cutover & sunset

Zero-downtime cutover during a planned maintenance window. PHP monolith decommissioned. I transitioned to ongoing Tech Lead role for the platform's maintenance and feature development phase.

05. Reflection

What I'd do differently.

Honest notes

We under-invested in observability for the first three months. By month four we had to retrofit distributed tracing and structured logging across services that were already in production — work that would have taken half the time if we'd built it in from the start.

I'd also have insisted earlier on a written architectural decision record (ADR) discipline. We made good decisions; we didn't always document why, and that cost us context when new engineers joined in months 8 and 12.

The Strangler Fig pattern worked. The team grew. The product never stopped shipping. But the parts that worked best were the parts where we paused to write things down.
